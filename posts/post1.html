My First Step into ML

February 22, 2026

When learning Machine Learning, you need a real-world project to put theory into practice. As my first project, I chose the Titanic dataset — a classic and convenient starting point for those just getting into ML.

The main goal of the project was to predict whether a passenger survived the Titanic based on their information (age, gender, ticket class, etc.). To do this, I built two classification models — Logistic Regression and Decision Tree, and compared them.

The dataset consists of 8 columns about 887 passengers: survival status, ticket class, name, gender, age, number of relatives, and price paid. I first dug deep into the data. I found several interesting patterns: the overall survival rate was only 38.56%. The gender gap is huge — 74.20% of women survived, while only 19.02% of men survived. Ticket class also played a significant role: 62.96% of 1st class passengers survived, 47.28% of 2nd class passengers survived, and 24.44% of 3rd class passengers survived.

Before training the models, I processed the data. I converted the text values ​​in the gender column to numbers, created a new feature to calculate family size, and selected the 6 most important columns for the models: ticket class, gender, age, price, family size, and single status.

I divided the data into 80% training and 20% test. The Logistic Regression model showed 76.40% accuracy. The Decision Tree model achieved 79.78% accuracy and was the winner. I visualized the results of both models using a confusion matrix and analyzed where they were making mistakes.

This project taught me not only how to write code, but also how to read data, make sense of it, and interpret the results. I also experienced the importance of Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA). If you don't understand the data well before building a model, the results won't be accurate.
